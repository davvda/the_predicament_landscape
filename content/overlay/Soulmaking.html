Soulmaking

Let's go for a walk. I am writing this on New Year's Eve of 2025, early morning, everyone still asleep. When a new year approaches I naturally begin reflecting on what theme could encapsulate my intentions and aspirations for the coming year. My theme for 2026 that crystallised just after Christmas is Year of the Householder. For now, I'll leave the reasoning behind the theme mostly unexplained. I will, however, expand a bit on how the notion of a householder relates to the external and internal world, a nonsensical but for this case useful distinction. Furthermore, let us see how this might relate, on a personal level, to Rob Burbea's notion of soulmaking.

A quick note before we jump in. Soulmaking, as understood through the work of Rob Burbea, is something I do not claim to fully understand and is, more importantly, not something I have practiced enough to have any right to explain to anyone else. Not that that has ever stopped me before. What follows here is nothing but a vague idea of what it might mean for me, in my life, at this particular moment. Vague ideas, however, are sometimes all that's needed to take another step.

In Buddhism, a householder is someone who is not a renunciate, someone not living as a monastic, someone who still lives and moves out in the world. We sometimes say that Zen practice is the turning of all arrows towards oneself. For a householder, there will always remain outward-pointing arrows. Another word for householder is layman. However, where layman is simply a practicitioner who isn't a monastic, a householder has active ties with the non-monastic world; work, family, economy, society, technology, politics, and more. So the household practitioner is out and about in the world, a participant in human affairs.

So the wise layman while being in this world, will try to be less and less of it. He will train his mind to look at life mindfully with detachment, and soon discover that modern civilization is, by and large, a commercial one, for the benefit of a powerful minority at the expense of the unthinking majority, based on the intensification and multiplication of artificial wants, often by arousing and stimulating the undesirable and lower elements of human nature, and that the increasing satisfaction of these wants leads not to peace and stillness of mind, but only to chronic discontent, restlessness, dissatisfaction, and conflict.
...
In order to awaken or to use our life in a skillful way, the first task is to get here, to start to live in the present moment, which means not living so much in our fantasies, in the future, not living so much in the past, in our images and memories, and reliving things that are gone already.

- Jack Kornfield

It bears repeating. In order to awaken to and use our life in a skillfull way, the first task is to get here. How one gets here is a lifelong practice in and of itself that, condensed to one of its essences, entails the practice of decreasing fabrication. Taking a leap across this stream, I hope you're following, we can then, as householders, see that there is, inwardly, the practice of decreasing fabrication and, outwardly, "using our life in a skillful way". Jack Kornfield again:

So there are two parts. The first is seeing what is here, living in the reality of the present, and then responding to it wisely, being aware of the situation that we're in.
...
For householders, which is what we are as a group, our main practice is virtue. You could say our main practice is awareness, but the main teachings are working with virtue, which means working with how we act in the world since we are not renunciates; how we speak and act with one another, to learn to be grounded in non-harming. In discovering the uprightness of heart and action, there's a real strength that comes to us.

- Jack Kornfield

Two parts. Get here, decrease fabrication, out there, be virtuous. Perhaps, before arriving at soulmaking, we should explore fabrication and the practices aimed at decreasing or affecting it. Definitionally, fabrication refers to the process of constructing or manufacturing something, typically by assembling various components or materials. It can also mean lying, misrepresenting, making up something out of nothing. I suppose another way of looking at it, for our purposes, is that fabrication is any mental construct or conception. The way humans practice with decreasing fabrication is through meditation. Working with mindfulness, bare attention(1), or any of the myriad meditation practices that exist allow you to, both instantly and gradually, get here. So, inwardly, meditation is critical. It is, in fact, nigh on a responsibility.

How do we then make sure that virtue grows out of this fertile soil? And what is virtue? Having gotten, and constantly showing up, truly here, we have already created the necessary conditions for a skillful use of our life, also called virtue. This virtue can then be reframed as nothing but skillful fabrication or, perhaps, fabrication with intent. Inwardly, decrease fabrication. Outwardly, skillful and virtuous fabrication with intent followed by the actions springing out from it. And here is where we arrive at soulmaking.

I feel we have a kind of cosmological and anthropological crisis. In other words, our ideas, our logoi, our ideas of what the cosmos is, what the world is, what matter is, what nature is, and our ideas of anthropos, of humankind, are poor, are thin, have reached their limit. And we see the effects now. Because of globalization and the massive power of technology, the effects of such limited and thin and not very fecund views of what a human being is, and what animals are, and what trees are, and what the world is, and what this cosmos is -- it gets flatly reduced in this scientific materialist or scientistic, even, view of our selves, of human beings. What a person is, what a human being is, what this world is, that needs some kind of expansion or even revolution.

In other words, what I'm saying is the typically flatly modernist, Western Enlightenment, Scientific Revolution-spawned view of the universe, of which we are the inheritors, as someone said to me, "in all its brilliance and all its brutality." We are the inheritors of that, and it has a tremendous amount of gifts and really a lot of costs. We are becoming more conscious of that. Carrying on with the same paradigm, with the same kind of views and conceptions and the same logos of what the world is, what nature is, what a human being is, it will not deliver, it will not give rise to the kinds of senses of reverence, and humility, and place, and awe, and duty, and blessedness, and participation that we need if we are to start treating the world, the world of nature, the world of other human beings, and ourselves -- let's just say better, or at least more interestingly.

- Rob Burbea

Soulmaking, then, is fabricating skillfully, wisely, virtuously. This allows us to revive and put soul into the world through the imagination. Creating beauty, conditions for life to manifest all its grandeur. Let your life be a soulfulness story told through your actions.

Soulmaking is in relationship with images and experiences, not in the images and the fantasies alone. It’s in relationship with, in the conscious working with – which includes the conceptual frameworks – the conscious working with, the relationship with the images and experiences, in that relating.

- Rob Burbea

What, then, makes something soulful? How does one make soul? What is this resonant relation with image and fantasy? One could spend many lifetimes pondering the question. Burbea identified the following qualities as the elements of the imaginal when sensing with the soul:

1. Trust
2. Beauty
3. Reverence
4. Imaginal Middle Way
5. Loving and Being Loved
6. Eternality
7. Dimensionality (shading into Divinity)
8. Soft and elastic edges
9. Concertina (other images waiting in the wings)
10. Soulfulness
11. Energy body awareness
12. Grace
13. Logos (ideas / concepts are involved)
14. Values (are involved)
15. Slightly less fabrication
16. Sense of Eternality
17. Both Created and Discovered
18. Autonomy of Self and Other
19. Unfathomable Beyonds
20. Meaningfulness
21. Fullness of Intention (to serve soul/soulfulness/the imaginal)
22. Participation
23. Duty
24. This Lattice of Elements
25. Not reducible to a single meaning
26. Infinite echoing and mirroring
27. Humility
28. Eros (or Loving Desire)

Strong resonance here. Rob Burbea died in 2020 but at certain points on the path I'm on, if I happen to be passing a spot at just the right time, sun breaking through the clouds, I see blinking reflections in mirror shards that have been placed on the mountain slopes.

As I recognize the presence of image and fantasy, and honour my need for that, and for soulfulness, as I do that more and more through practice, and get a feel for it, it changes my vision, literally. It changes my ways of looking and perceiving things, and I begin to recognize the fantasies in everything that I care about. Everything that’s meaningful to me, I recognize it’s shot through with fantasy, with image, with mythos. It’s imbuing everything that I love.

- Rob Burbea

Coming back to here. 2026 will be, for me, the Year of the Householder. My meditation practice will deepen but, more importantly, regularly and through rigorous practice arriving here, I will deepen the practices of soulmaking that can lead me to acting wisely and virtuously in the world, for myself, my family, friends, animals, all beings. That, to me, is the true lifelong work of the householder bodhisattva, the living of the vows.

Beings are numberless, I vow to free them.
Delusions are inexhaustible, I vow to transform them.
Reality is boundless, I vow to perceive it.
The awakened way is unsurpassable, I vow to embody it.

Onward.


(1) Now, Burbea rightly points out that bare attention is rarely, if ever, truly bare. There are always conceptions and assumptions even if you've stripped the attention of much of the fluff. Mindfulness, or the practice of bare attention, is a critical tool for life. Another, even more fabrication-decreasing practice, something I have very limited practice with, Burbea calls emptiness practice. These are practices with the direct intent of decreasing fabrication. I know too little about this to confidently describe or explain it. In fact, if I held myself to the standard of confident explanations, none of this would've ever been written.

There's no perception without a way of looking. What we can regard meditation as is a playing with, a skilful playing with and experimenting with, a range of ways of looking.

- Rob Burbea
