Being reasonable

There are so many different kinds of stupidity, and cleverness is one of the worst.


Thomas Mann

To be reasonable is, or rather should be, to think through and to understand. Most features of our predicament landscape have arisen due to our commitment to (try to) be reasonable. Unreasonably reasonable. To stop deforestation is not seen as reasonable, to cut down most of the old growth forests apparently is. Reasoning has its merits but it seems we must dare to be a bit unreasonable to be able to manage our modern predicaments. All reasoning tends to follow a certain optimisation and prioritisation and if we haven't figured out what to optimise for (meta-optimisation) our reasoning has little value.

I've been cordially invited to join the visceral realists. I accepted, of course. There was no initiation ceremony. It was better that way.


Roberto Bolaño, The Savage Detectives

Outgoing connections:

  • Business-as-usual narrative - Drastically changing course is often seen as unreasonable.
  • Failure to take responsibility - There is an unreasonable amount of things, past and present, that we must take responsibility for.
  • Hemispherical asymmetry (Brain) - The left hemisphere deals with narrow reasoning, unaware of how narrow it is.
  • Ignorance of the mystical - You cannot reason with the mystical.
  • Ignorance of the mythical - The power of myths lie deeper than reason.
  • Ignored externalities - If you decide to factor in the externalities of modernity you quickly discover that nothing is reasonable.
  • Ignoring complexity - Making sense of complexity with reason alone is not feasible. Therefore, in order to make sense of it with reason, we frequently just don't. A wholly reasonable partial explanation is often more attractive than a partially reasonable whole explanation.
  • Illusion of no option - Sticking to a path that seems reasonable in a landscape made largely unreasonable becomes a struggle to stay on the path that promised us the future, with no reasonable options remaining to actually get there.
  • Illusion of understanding - Reason has little to do with understanding. They are distant relatives.
  • Immoral justifications - Someone skilled in reasoning can justify anything.
  • Map and territory - You do not reason with reality, but you're free to doodle on the map as much as you like.
  • Mind-body separation - Reasonable people tend to think they're a brain living inside of a body.
  • Misinformation - Whatever sounds reasonable will most likely be propagated through media or the grapevine.
  • Rationalism - The sect of reason is often called rationalism.
  • Scientism - The religion of reason has become scientism.
  • Sense-making crisis - Reason is not sufficient to make sense of our predicament.
  • Simplifications - Reasoning needs simplified models to make sense of the world.
  • Wisdom crisis - Our obsession with reasoning might be our main obstacle to wisdom.
